---

## Section 3: BUSINESS REGISTRATION

**Headline:**
Business Registration

**Body:**
The Ghana Investment Promotion Centre (GIPC) governs the registration of foreign and jointly held businesses. Minimum capital requirements and sector-specific restrictions apply, and these vary depending on your business activity and ownership structure.

A verified corporate attorney or GIPC-registered agent can manage the entire registration process — from document preparation and notarisation through to certificate issuance. Attempting to navigate GIPC registration without professional support is possible but time-consuming. Most established operators use an agent.

**Inline CTA:** Find a verified business registration agent →

---

## Section 4: STATUTORY COMPLIANCE

**Headline:**
Statutory Compliance

**Body:**
All operating businesses in Ghana are subject to oversight from several statutory bodies. The Ghana Revenue Authority (GRA) governs tax registration, returns, and filing obligations. The Social Security and National Insurance Trust (SSNIT) administers mandatory employer and employee contributions. Sector-specific licensing requirements apply from bodies including the Ghana Tourism Authority (GTA), the Ghana Immigration Service (GIS), and the Food and Drugs Authority (FDA) where relevant.

Non-compliance carries penalties that are disproportionate relative to the administrative effort required to stay current. A qualified accountant or compliance officer with Ghana-market experience is a sound early investment.

**Inline CTA:** Find a verified corporate accountant →

---

## Section 5: DUE DILIGENCE

**Headline:**
Due Diligence

**Body:**
Before entering any joint venture, acquiring property, or signing a commercial lease in Ghana, independent due diligence is essential. Land title disputes, undisclosed encumbrances, and misrepresented ownership structures are among the most common and most costly errors made by international entrants to the Ghanaian market.

Our directory lists verified corporate advisory and legal professionals who specialise in Ghana-market transactions and have been reviewed by the Communly team.

Every expat and returnee arrives in Accra with the same mix of excitement and overwhelm. ## Section 3: THE FIRST 24 HOURS

**Headline:**
The First 24 Hours

**Body:**
The moment you land at Kotoka International Airport, your priorities are: a working SIM card, Ghanaian cedis in your pocket, and a confirmed address to sleep at that night. Everything else can wait.

**SIM Card**
The major networks — MTN, AirtelTigo, and Telecel Ghana — all have booths inside and outside the arrivals hall. Buy a SIM from any of them immediately. Data is inexpensive and essential. MTN has the strongest coverage outside Accra.

Registration is required under Ghanaian law. You will need your passport. Staff at the booth will assist with activation. Top up with Mobile Money from day one — it is how Ghana pays for almost everything.

**Currency**
The Ghanaian cedi (GHS) is the local currency. USD is widely accepted for large transactions, particularly for rent, school fees, and high-value purchases — but you need cedis for daily life. Use an ATM inside the airport or at any major bank on your first day. Notify your home bank before travel to avoid card blocks.

**Accommodation**
If you do not yet have permanent accommodation confirmed, book a vetted short-stay serviced apartment for your first week. Do not attempt to solve the housing question from a hotel lobby on day two while managing jet lag. The GhanaExpats Property section lists verified options available immediately.

The First Week

**Body:**
Once you have your SIM, your cedis, and a base, the first week is about orientation. Not decisions — orientation.

**Getting Around**
Accra does not have a reliable public transport network by European or North American standards. **Opening a Bank Account**
You will need a Ghanaian bank account for day-to-day transactions, receiving payments, and eventually paying rent. GTBank, Ecobank, and Absa Ghana are commonly used by expats. Budget three to five working days for the account to be fully active.

**Registering with Your Embassy**
Register with your home country's embassy or high commission within your first week. It is a simple online form for most nationalities. In the event of a genuine emergency — civil unrest, a medical crisis, natural disaster — your embassy can only help people it knows are in the country.

## Section 2: INTRODUCTION

**Headline:**
The Accra rental market rewards preparation.

**Body:**
Finding housing in Accra as an expat or diaspora returnee is a process unlike anything you will have experienced in Europe, North America, or most other African capitals. The market is fast-moving, document-heavy, and cash-advance-intensive. A desirable property at the right price does not sit on the market for long.

This guide covers how to search effectively, which neighbourhoods suit different lifestyles and budgets, how to work with agents, what the standard lease terms look like, and the mistakes most first-time renters make. Reading it before you start searching will save you money and significant stress.

---

## Section 3: NEIGHBOURHOODS

**Headline:**
Where to Live in Accra

**Body:**
Accra is not a single neighbourhood. It is a sprawling metropolitan area of distinct communities, each with its own character, price point, and practical profile.

**East Legon & Trasacco**
The preferred address for international families, corporate assignees, and senior professionals. Well-maintained private roads, proximity to most international schools and the Airport Residential Area, and a density of high-quality restaurants and leisure facilities. Premium pricing reflects the demand.

**Airport Residential Area**
Central, secure, and close to the major private hospitals and international embassies. A logical choice for those whose work involves frequent travel or contact with international institutions. Supply is limited and competition is high.

**Cantonments & Roman Ridge**
Established, leafy, and well-located. Popular with NGO staff, diplomatic personnel, and mid-to-senior corporate tenants. Quieter than East Legon but with good access to central Accra.

**Labone & Osu**
For those who want to be near the cultural energy of the city — restaurants, galleries, nightlife — without the full East Legon premium. A popular choice for younger professionals and creative industry workers.

**Tema**
Ghana's industrial and port city, 25 kilometres east of Accra. Purpose-built, grid-structured, and notably quieter than the capital. Relevant primarily for those whose work is port or logistics-connected. Commuting to central Accra is feasible but time-consuming during peak hours.

## Section 4: HOW AGENTS WORK

**Headline:**
Working with Agents in Accra

**Body:**
Most medium-to-high-end rentals in Accra are handled by estate agents. The agent market in Ghana is partially regulated — some agents are members of formal bodies such as the Ghana Real Estate Developers Association (GREDA), but many operate independently. This is not inherently a problem, but it makes verification important.

**Agency fees** are typically paid by the tenant, not the landlord. The standard commission is equivalent to one month's rent per year of the lease. On a two-year lease, expect to pay the equivalent of two months' rent to the agent.

**Advance rent** is the most significant financial hurdle for new arrivals. Two years' advance rent is common for quality properties in preferred neighbourhoods. One year is occasionally negotiable. Monthly tenancies are rare for anything above entry-level accommodation. Plan your cash position accordingly.

## Section 5: LEASE TERMS

**Headline:**
Understanding Your Lease

**Body:**
Ghanaian residential leases are generally straightforward but contain clauses that differ from European or North American norms. The following are the most important points to understand before signing.

**Advance rent payment** — as above. Confirm the payment structure and currency before viewing. Properties in premium areas are frequently priced in USD and require USD payment.

**Maintenance responsibilities** — clarify in writing who is responsible for generator maintenance, water supply, exterior repairs, and major appliance failure. Ambiguity on this point is the source of most landlord-tenant disputes.

**Early termination** — most Ghanaian leases do not include a break clause. Exiting a lease early typically requires negotiation and may result in forfeiting some or all of the advance rent paid.

**Generator and water supply** — confirm availability, ownership, and maintenance responsibility for any backup generator and water storage on the property. These are not luxuries in Accra — they are operational necessities.

**Get everything in writing.** Verbal agreements with landlords are not enforceable. Any commitment made by a landlord or agent that is not in the signed lease does not exist legally.

## Section 6: COMMON MISTAKES

**Headline:**
The Mistakes Most New Arrivals Make

**Body:**
Every year, expats and returnees arriving in Accra lose money, time, and goodwill on housing decisions that were entirely avoidable. These are the most common.

Paying advance rent before viewing the property in person — always view physically before any payment. Photography can misrepresent condition significantly.

Failing to verify the landlord's right to rent the property — ask for proof of ownership or a notarised authority to let. Your agent should supply this as standard.

Assuming estate agent fees are negotiable downwards before you have found your property — they are sometimes, but flagging price sensitivity before a property search is identified often causes agents to deprioritise your requirements.

Signing a lease in a language you cannot read without a certified translation — if the lease is in Twi or any other language you do not read fluently, obtain a certified English translation before signing.

The school your children attend will significantly influence where in Accra you choose to live — not the other way around. ## Section 3: CURRICULUM OPTIONS

**Headline:**
Choosing the Right Curriculum

**British curriculum (IGCSE & A-levels)**
The most widely available international curriculum in Ghana. Schools following the British pathway lead to internationally recognised GCSE and A-level qualifications. Well-suited to families who may return to the UK or whose children may pursue higher education in the UK, Commonwealth countries, or internationally.

**International Baccalaureate (IB)**
The IB Diploma Programme is offered at a small number of schools in Ghana and is widely recognised by universities across North America, Europe, and beyond. The IB is academically demanding and particularly well-suited to highly mobile families who anticipate multiple country relocations.

**American curriculum**
American-curriculum schools prepare students for US university admission, including SAT and AP examinations. A good option for families with US university aspirations or those likely to relocate to North America.

**Ghanaian national curriculum with international recognition**
Several schools following the Ghana Education Service curriculum have achieved international accreditation and produce graduates who successfully enter international universities. Typically lower fees than fully international institutions.

---

## Section 4: ADMISSION TIMELINES

**Headline:**
Admission Timelines and What to Expect

**Body:**
The most important thing to understand about international school admissions in Ghana is that the most desirable schools maintain waiting lists, and the academic calendar does not accommodate late arrivals. Begin the application process at least six months before your expected arrival date where possible.

The academic year in Ghana typically begins in September, mirroring the British system. Applications for September entry are competitive from January onwards. Mid-year entry is possible at most schools but subject to place availability.

Required documents across most institutions include: birth certificate, previous school records and transcripts, vaccination records, copy of passport, and a parent or guardian letter of intent. Some schools conduct an assessment or interview as part of the process.

School Fees: What to Budget

International school fees in Ghana vary significantly by institution, curriculum, and year group. The following ranges are indicative for the 2025/26 academic year and should be confirmed directly with each school.

## Section 4: INTERNATIONAL HEALTH INSURANCE

**Headline:**
Health Insurance: What You Need

**Body:**
A comprehensive international health insurance policy is not optional for expats in Ghana. The cost of private emergency care, evacuation, or specialist treatment without coverage can be financially devastating.

Your policy should cover, at minimum: inpatient and outpatient private care, emergency medical evacuation to your home country or a regional medical hub (South Africa is the most common for Ghana-based evacuation), dental care, and prescription medication. **Body:**
Major pharmacy chains operating in Accra include Ernest Chemists, Pharmanova, and Qualiphar — all of which operate multiple branches across the city and maintain generally reliable stock of common medications.

For specialist or branded prescription medication not available in Ghana, the most reliable options are: prescription delivery from South Africa via medical courier (a service several private hospitals and specialist clinics can arrange), or bringing a sufficient supply from your home country on arrival.

Controlled substances — including certain psychiatric medications and opioid-based pain relief — are subject to import restrictions under Ghanaian law. Check the regulatory position on any medication before travelling.
